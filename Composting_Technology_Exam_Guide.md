## 3. Deep content notes by topic

### A. Temperature phases (the compost curve)
- **Mesophilic (Stage 1):** ~20–40°C. Mesophilic bacteria break down sugars, starches, proteins; pile temperature rises; some bacteria form endospores as heat builds.
- **Thermophilic (Stage 2):** ~40–65°C. Thermophilic bacteria/fungi take over; mesophiles go dormant; **pathogens and weed seeds are destroyed**; complex carbohydrates and hemicellulose break down; temperature peaks here.
- **Curing/Maturation (Stage 3, "Psychrophilic"):** <40°C. Mesophiles return; metabolic activity slows; **lignin** (most resistant) is broken down by actinomycetes and fungi; temperature drops toward ambient.
- Physical decomposers (earthworms, mites, springtails, nematodes, etc.) support the process by shredding material and exposing new surfaces to microbes.
- **X-axis = time (days); Y-axis = temperature (°C).** The *theoretical* curve is smooth; the *actual* curve has repeated dips — each dip = a turning/agitation event that temporarily cools the pile, followed by re-heating as microbes recover.
- Large particle size → slower heat build-up, lower peak temperature, more uneven heating (less surface area for microbial attack).

### B. C:N ratio
- Ideal starting C:N ratio ≈ **25–30:1**. Too high (excess carbon, e.g. dry leaves/sawdust) → slow decomposition, nitrogen-limited microbial growth. Too low (excess nitrogen, e.g. manure) → nitrogen lost as ammonia gas, odour problems.
- Balance "browns" (high-C: straw, dried leaves, sawdust) with "greens" (high-N: manure, food scraps, fresh grass).
- Link this to raw-material-selection questions — always mention C:N balancing when discussing mixing raw materials.

### C. Particle size, porosity, bulk density, FAS
- **Smaller particles** = more surface area = faster microbial attack, but **too small** = poor porosity = compaction = anaerobic pockets.
- **Larger/irregular/rigid particles** (e.g., twigs, straw) create structure and porosity — hold open pore spaces for airflow even under the pile's own weight.
- **Porosity** = void space in the pile that can hold air or water. **Free Air Space (FAS)** = the portion of that void space actually occupied by air (not water) — ideal FAS is roughly 30–36%.
- **FAS decreases with pile depth** — deeper layers are compacted by the weight above, squeezing out air space, so the bottom of a tall pile is more prone to anaerobic conditions than the top.
- **Bulk density = mass ÷ volume.** Denser packing (uniform, tightly-fitting round particles with little structural material — like pile "C" in the 2022 paper's figure) = highest bulk density, lowest porosity, slowest, most anaerobic-prone decomposition. A mix of irregular shapes and varied sizes (like a mix of rods and spheres) gives good structure and quicker decomposition.
- **Chimney effect:** as the pile heats, warm air rises and escapes through the top, drawing cooler fresh air in through the base/sides passively (convective air flow) — this is the mechanism behind *passive* aeration in static piles, without mechanical turning.
- **Why too much agitation is bad:** (1) excessive heat loss/cooling can drop the pile below thermophilic temperatures needed to kill pathogens; (2) excessive drying — turning exposes moisture to air and can dry the pile below the ~40–60% moisture needed for microbial activity, slowing decomposition.

### D. Composting methods — know the difference between all of these
| Method | Key feature |
|---|---|
| **Bangalore method (traditional)** | Anaerobic; pit-based; minimal turning |
| **Indore method (traditional)** | Aerobic via passive aeration; done as pit or heap; layered raw materials, occasional turning |
| **Windrow composting** | Long narrow rows, periodically turned mechanically or manually |
| **Aerated Static Pile (ASP)** | One large pile, not turned; perforated pipes/bulking agents allow passive or blown airflow from base to top |
| **In-vessel composting** | Enclosed drum/silo/trench; environment (temp, moisture, aeration) tightly controlled; usually has built-in turning/agitation mechanism (e.g. auger) |
| **Tumbler** | A small rotating drum version of in-vessel, good for limited home/urban space, but lower yield |
| **Vermicomposting** | Earthworms process organic material into castings; suited to small-scale/urban settings |

For any "design a strategic plan" question: pick a method, justify by raw material availability, land/space, capital, labour, and climate — this is exactly the structure of Q1(c) 2018, Q1(iii) 2019, Q4 2022, Q3 2023-Jan.

### E. Vermicomposting — five sub-topics the 2019 & 2022 papers explicitly list
1. **Gut-associated vs cast-associated processes**: gut-associated = microbial and enzymatic digestion happening inside the earthworm's gut as it ingests organic matter; cast-associated = further microbial activity that continues in the excreted casts after passing through the worm, which are rich in humic substances and available nutrients.
2. **Positive/negative effects of earthworms on microbial biomass**: positive — worms fragment material, increase surface area, spread and stimulate beneficial microbial populations through gut passage; negative — intense grazing can also reduce standing microbial biomass by consuming microorganisms as a food source.
3. **Environmental factors affecting earthworm survival/growth**: moisture (~60–80%), temperature (mesophilic range, ~20–30°C — earthworms die at high thermophilic temperatures, unlike thermophilic composting), pH (near neutral), aeration, and food/organic matter availability.
4. **Species suitability**: species like *Eisenia fetida* (and locally, species referenced in your papers such as *Dichogaster* and *Amynthas* species) vary in their suitability for vermicomposting and soil improvement.
5. **Physical/chemical characteristics of vermicompost**: dark, crumbly, humus-like texture; near-neutral pH; high in available N, P, K and micronutrients; high cation exchange capacity; low C:N ratio compared to raw feedstock.

### F. Biofertilizer
- **Definition:** a product containing living microorganisms (N-fixing bacteria, P-solubilizing bacteria/fungi, mycorrhizae, etc.) that, when applied to seed/soil/plant, colonize the rhizosphere and promote growth by increasing nutrient availability.
- **Key organism groups from your slides**: N₂-fixing mycorrhizae, P-solubilizing bacteria, P-solubilizing fungi.
- **Preparation factors:** carrier material selection, moisture content, viable microbial load/shelf life, sterility of carrier, storage temperature, pH.
- **As a soil conditioner vs inorganic fertilizer:** improves soil structure/biology long-term rather than only supplying immediate nutrients; slower-acting, lower nutrient concentration than chemical fertilizer, but improves long-term soil health, reduces chemical runoff, and is renewable — a "critically assess" question wants you to weigh yield speed (inorganic wins short-term) vs sustainability (organic/bio wins long-term), and note that most modern Sri Lankan practice is **integrated use of both**, not full substitution.

### G. SLS Standards (be ready to name-drop the standard numbers)
- **SLS 1635:2019 — Specification for Compost**
- **SLS 1702:2021 — Specification for Liquid Organic Fertilizers** (approved 24 March 2021)
- **SLS 1704:2021 — Specification for Sterilized Solid Organic Fertilizer** (approved 25 May 2021)
- **Why standards exist:** to guarantee product safety (pathogen-free, no heavy metals/contaminants above threshold), guarantee minimum nutrient content so farmers get value, enable trust/market access, and support regulation during the government's organic fertilizer policy push.
- **What they typically specify:** physical requirements (moisture, particle size, colour/odour), chemical requirements (pH, EC, N/P/K minimums, C:N ratio, heavy metal limits), biological requirements (pathogen absence), labelling requirements (panel layout for bags/sachets).

### H. Site & environmental considerations for a composting plant
- Site selection criteria: adequate area, all-weather access, distance from residences/water bodies, wind direction, drainage.
- **Separation distances** from sensitive receptors (homes, water sources, roads).
- **Drainage requirements** to prevent leachate contaminating groundwater/surface water.
- **Buffer zone** importance: odour/noise/vector control, aesthetic/social acceptance buffer, environmental protection margin.
- Environmental considerations: leachate management, odour control, pest/vector control, greenhouse gas management, noise.
- Socio-economic backgrounds to weigh: community acceptance, employment generation, land use conflicts, cost recovery/business model.

### I. Mechanization
- **Advantages**: consistent/faster turning, reduced labour dependency (directly answers the "labourer shortage" 2022 exam scenario), better aeration control, larger throughput, more consistent product quality, reduced processing time.
- **Machinery by process step**: shredders/grinders (size reduction) → mixers/loaders (blending raw materials) → turners/augers or in-vessel drums (aeration/agitation) → screens/trommels (final product sizing/screening) → bagging/packaging equipment.
- Flow chart structure to memorize: **Waste receiving → Sorting → Shredding → Mixing (C:N adjustment) → Active composting (turning/aeration) → Curing → Screening → Packaging/Storage.**

### J. Raw materials — what CANNOT be composted, and why
- Non-biodegradable/inert waste: plastics, glass, metal, e-waste — don't decompose, contaminate final product.
- Treated/chemically-hazardous waste: chemically treated wood, materials with heavy metals, medical/hazardous waste, materials with high pesticide/herbicide residues — contaminate compost, harmful to crops/humans/animals if the compost is later applied to farmland (bioaccumulation, phytotoxicity, food safety risk).
- Diseased plant material and pet/human faeces (biosafety/pathogen risk) unless properly thermophilically treated.
- **Impacts if used anyway on cropland**: heavy-metal/contaminant accumulation in soil and crops, phytotoxicity reducing yield, food safety risk to consumers (bioaccumulation up the food chain), soil degradation over time, groundwater contamination via leachate.

### K. Novel/rapid technologies
- **EM (Effective Microorganism)-based quick composting**: inoculating the pile with a consortium of beneficial microbes (lactic acid bacteria, yeasts, phototrophic bacteria) to accelerate decomposition and suppress pathogens/odour.
- **Berkeley rapid composting method**: a highly managed, frequently-turned hot composting method (typically 18-day cycle) using a well-balanced C:N mix, small particle size, and very frequent turning to maintain thermophilic temperatures and speed up composting dramatically compared to passive methods.
- **Auger mixing systems** (in in-vessel composting): mechanically agitate/mix material continuously or on a schedule — pros: more uniform aeration and moisture distribution, faster processing, less manual labour, more consistent temperature control than a static pile.

### L. Merits & demerits of composting technology (from Lecture 10)
- **Environmental**: reduces landfill waste and methane emissions, lowers greenhouse gas footprint versus dumping.
- **Soil health**: nutrient-rich amendment, improves soil structure and water retention.
- **Waste/resource recovery**: converts waste into a usable resource, reduces dependency on chemical fertilizer, diverts yard/kitchen waste from landfills.
- **Economic**: low-cost, accessible at both household and community/industrial scale.
- **Demerits/considerations** (be ready to name a few): slower nutrient release than chemical fertilizer, requires land/space and time investment, potential odour/pest/leachate issues if poorly managed, variable nutrient content batch-to-batch, labour/technical-knowledge requirement for quality control.

## 4. High-yield exam-writing strategy

1. **Always structure with headers/sub-points** — examiners give partial marks per correctly identified point; a bulleted, labelled answer scores better than a wall of prose under time pressure.
2. **For "critically discuss/assess" sub-questions (usually the biggest mark allocation)**: use a **3-part structure** — (i) state the claim, (ii) give 2–3 supporting arguments, (iii) give 1–2 limitations/counterpoints, (iv) conclude with a balanced, Sri-Lanka-specific recommendation.
3. **Sketch when asked** — several questions explicitly award marks for a labelled diagram/flow chart (compost temperature curve, pit vs heap pipe installation, mechanization flow chart, volume-variance graph). Practice drawing these from memory; a correct sketch is often 10–20% of a question's marks on its own.
4. **Always tie back to Sri Lanka** — mention real context: traditional farmers' capital/land constraints, the 2021 100% organic agriculture policy and its fallout, chemical fertilizer import restrictions, government programmes (Pilisaru waste management programme, "Api Wawamu Rata Nagamu" home gardening programme), Western Province's ~7,000 MT/day waste generation figure, and SLSI standards (SLS 1635/1702/1704).
5. **Time budget**: with the 2022/2023 format (2 hrs total, Part A = 1 hr worth ~100 marks structured, Part B = 1 hr for 3×100 marks essays), spend roughly **1 minute per mark** — don't overwrite a 6-mark list question.
